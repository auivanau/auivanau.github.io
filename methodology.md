<p class="page-intro">The value of a surveillance digest depends less on the number of papers collected than on transparent selection, calibrated interpretation, and accurate representation of the original evidence.</p>

## Purpose

The weekly publications are designed to help clinicians and trainees notice consequential developments while avoiding indiscriminate abstract aggregation. They are **best-effort surveillance products**, not systematic reviews, clinical practice guidelines, or substitutes for reading the original paper.

## Publications and scope

### Radiation Oncology Weekly

The radiation oncology scan prioritizes:

- practice-changing oncology trials and guidelines;
- adaptive radiotherapy, CBCT, image guidance, and dose accumulation;
- contouring, planning, quality assurance, and workflow;
- clinically relevant AI, radiomics, and toxicity prediction;
- disease-site evidence with direct radiation oncology implications;
- medical physics innovations with plausible clinical utility.

Common sources include PubMed, major oncology and radiation oncology journals, Medical Physics, Physics in Medicine & Biology, and selected preprint servers. The exact sources searched are stated in each issue.

### Clinical Horizon Scan

The general medical scan prioritizes developments with potential implications for clinical decisions, patient outcomes, public health, regulation, or healthcare delivery. It includes major trials, regulatory actions, biotechnology, diagnostics, negative evidence, and clinically relevant AI.

## Selection principles

Items are selected using four questions:

1. **Could this change a clinical decision, outcome, workflow, or research direction?**
2. **How strong and mature is the evidence?**
3. **How broadly applicable is the finding?**
4. **Is the result genuinely new, or mainly a technical or promotional signal?**

Novelty alone is not enough. A negative randomized trial may rank above a highly accurate retrospective model because it provides more reliable guidance for patient care.

## Ranking framework

Radiation oncology papers are grouped as:

- **Must Read:** high clinical importance, strong evidence, or an unusually consequential practice or workflow signal;
- **Worth Skimming:** relevant and credible, but narrower, preliminary, or unlikely to change immediate practice;
- **Save for Later:** potentially useful for a specific disease site, method, or future project;
- **Low Priority:** limited incremental value, weak validation, unclear decision impact, or relevance mainly outside routine practice.

The numerical importance score is an editorial aid rather than a validated instrument. It reflects a combination of clinical consequence, evidence strength, novelty, generalizability, and implementation readiness.

## Evidence maturity

Each appraisal distinguishes among evidence types, including:

- randomized or prospective clinical evidence;
- observational or retrospective clinical evidence;
- guidelines, consensus statements, and systematic reviews;
- translational, technical, or dosimetric studies;
- preprints and early feasibility work;
- regulatory or public-health announcements.

A peer-reviewed label does not guarantee high-quality evidence, and a preprint label does not imply that a finding is unimportant. The labels help readers calibrate confidence.

## Use of AI

AI tools may assist with broad search, deduplication, extraction, organization, and drafting. Editorial judgment remains necessary for inclusion, ranking, interpretation, limitation assessment, and clinical framing. Whenever feasible, claims are checked against primary sources or authoritative regulatory and public-health sources.

AI-assisted surveillance has predictable failure modes: missed papers, incorrect dates, citation errors, overconfident summaries, and failure to recognize clinically important context. Readers should use the linked original sources when a finding may affect practice or scholarship.

## Conflicts, independence, and privacy

The Lab is an independent personal publication. No article should be interpreted as institutional guidance. Financial relationships or relevant conflicts will be disclosed when applicable. Patient-identifying information is not intentionally published.

## Corrections

Substantive errors should be corrected in the article as soon as they are identified. Material corrections should be accompanied by a dated note explaining what changed. Minor spelling, formatting, or link repairs may be made silently.

Corrections or concerns can be raised through the public contact channels linked from the site’s GitHub profile.

## Responsible use

The publications are educational. They do not provide individualized medical advice and should not be used as the sole basis for patient-care decisions. Clinicians should consult the full evidence, applicable guidelines, local policies, and relevant specialists.
