## What I write about

The Lab is a professional working notebook and independent publication focused on four overlapping areas:

- radiation oncology and clinically important cancer evidence;
- medical physics, imaging, and adaptive radiotherapy;
- practical uses and limitations of artificial intelligence in medicine;
- engineering, design, and health-system problem solving.

The flagship publication, **[Radiation Oncology Weekly]({{ '/radonc-weekly/' | relative_url }})**, ranks and critically appraises newly published work for clinicians, trainees, physicists, and researchers. **[Clinical Horizon Scan]({{ '/clinical-horizon-scan/' | relative_url }})** looks beyond oncology to identify important cross-specialty medical developments.

## Background

Before medicine, I studied materials engineering and worked in nanomaterials research in Germany and in Alberta’s energy sector, including oil-sands operations, failure analysis, and pipeline integrity. Those experiences shaped how I approach clinical problems: define the system, identify failure modes, separate measurements from outcomes, and make uncertainty visible.

I use this site to document literature surveillance, evidence appraisal, prototypes, workflows, and questions that emerge while training in radiation oncology.

## Editorial independence

The views expressed here are my own. They do not represent the University of Alberta, Alberta Health Services, the Cross Cancer Institute, or any other institution. The site is educational and is not medical advice. I aim to link primary sources, distinguish peer-reviewed evidence from preprints, identify important limitations, and correct substantive errors transparently.

For more detail, read the **[editorial methodology and correction policy]({{ '/methodology/' | relative_url }})**.

Outside medicine and engineering, I play drums, lift, read, and occasionally climb things.
