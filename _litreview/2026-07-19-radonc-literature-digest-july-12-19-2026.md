# Weekly Radiation Oncology Literature Surveillance Digest: July 12–19, 2026

**Coverage window searched:** July 12–19, 2026

**Scope searched:** PubMed, arXiv, medRxiv, bioRxiv, IJROBP, Radiotherapy & Oncology, Practical Radiation Oncology, JCO and ASCO publications, Lancet Oncology, Nature Medicine, Medical Physics, and Physics in Medicine & Biology.

**Note:** This is a best-effort literature surveillance digest, not a formal systematic review. Online-first publication dates were prioritized. Papers appearing in a July issue but released online before this surveillance window were generally excluded.

---

## Bottom Line This Week

The most clinically meaningful paper is the phase II randomized study of **oral spermidine supplementation for preservation of salivary-gland function after head and neck radiotherapy**. It is an intriguing supportive-care signal with a relatively low implementation burden, but the trial requires close review of sample size, blinding, adherence, toxicity reporting, and the clinical importance of the measured salivary outcomes before practice adoption.

The most conceptually novel clinical paper is the prostate-radiotherapy study evaluating an interaction between **PER3 circadian genotype and treatment time**. The work supports the broader idea that treatment timing may influence late radiation toxicity, but it remains biomarker-driven and hypothesis-generating rather than ready for routine scheduling decisions.

The most operationally relevant physics paper is the deep-learning study of **nonstop gated CBCT reconstruction for respiratory-gated pancreatic radiotherapy**. Faster or more complete gated volumetric imaging could improve motion-managed workflows, but image-quality metrics and technical feasibility are not substitutes for validation of registration accuracy, target localization, dose calculation, and clinical safety.

The review of **local prostate-cancer reirradiation** is useful for practice because it consolidates prospective evidence and planning considerations in an increasingly common but high-risk scenario. The evidence base remains heterogeneous, and careful selection, restaging, prior-dose reconstruction, urinary-function assessment, and centre-specific expertise remain essential.

Nothing published this week clearly establishes a new universal standard of care. Most findings are implementation-focused, supportive-care oriented, or hypothesis-generating. Overall, this was a moderately useful week, with more value for clinical nuance and workflow development than for immediate changes to definitive radiation prescriptions.

## Must Read

### 1. Oral Spermidine Supplementation Preserves Submandibular Gland Function After Radiotherapy

Citation: Min Y, Gao K, Liu Y, Dai L, Peng X. Oral Spermidine Supplementation Preserves Submandibular Gland Function After Radiotherapy: Mechanistic Insights and Use in a Phase II Randomized Clinical Trial. MedComm. 2026;7(8):e70862.[^1]

Journal/source: MedComm  
Publication type: Peer-reviewed phase II randomized clinical trial with translational mechanistic work  
One-line takeaway: Oral spermidine was evaluated as a low-burden intervention to preserve salivary-gland function after head and neck radiotherapy.

Evidence tier: Randomized phase II clinical evidence supported by mechanistic experiments; promising but not confirmatory.

Importance score: 8/10

Key limitations:

- Phase II evidence is insufficient by itself to establish a supportive-care standard.
- The clinical importance of the salivary endpoints, xerostomia outcomes, and duration of benefit requires careful review.
- Generalizability may depend on tumour site, salivary-gland dose, concurrent systemic therapy, baseline gland function, and adherence.
- Supplement-related interactions, long-term safety, and reproducibility in other populations remain uncertain.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Radiation-related xerostomia remains a major survivorship issue despite parotid-sparing IMRT. A safe oral intervention would be attractive because it could complement, rather than replace, salivary-gland dose reduction. This study is worth reading in full before changing practice, particularly to determine whether patient-reported xerostomia and clinically meaningful salivary recovery improved alongside mechanistic endpoints.

### 2. Circadian Genotypes in PER3 and Time of Prostate Cancer Radiotherapy Interact to Affect Risk of Late Side Effects

Citation: Webb AJ, et al. Circadian genotypes in PER3 and time of prostate cancer radiotherapy interact to affect risk of late side-effects. Clinical Cancer Research. Published online July 13, 2026.[^2]

Journal/source: Clinical Cancer Research  
Publication type: Peer-reviewed biomarker and clinical-outcomes study  
One-line takeaway: PER3 circadian genotype appeared to modify the relationship between treatment time and late toxicity after prostate radiotherapy.

Evidence tier: Observational translational study using clinical toxicity and germline biomarker data.

Importance score: 7/10

Key limitations:

- Observational interaction analyses are vulnerable to false-positive findings and residual confounding.
- Treatment time may correlate with centre, workflow, patient characteristics, fractionation, and other unmeasured factors.
- Genotype-defined scheduling requires independent validation before clinical use.
- The feasibility and equity implications of genotype-directed appointment allocation are unresolved.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
The study is important because treatment timing is modifiable and potentially inexpensive. However, this should not prompt routine morning-versus-afternoon scheduling based on PER3 genotype. Its immediate value is in supporting prospective chronoradiotherapy trials with prespecified toxicity endpoints and external validation.

## Worth Skimming

### 3. Breast Cancer Radiotherapy in Patients With Autoimmune or Connective Tissue Disease

Citation: Breast cancer radiotherapy in patients with autoimmune or connective tissue diseases. Radiation Oncology. Published online July 16, 2026.[^3]

Journal/source: Radiation Oncology  
Publication type: Peer-reviewed clinical outcomes study  
One-line takeaway: Contemporary breast radiotherapy outcomes were examined in a population historically considered at increased risk of radiation toxicity.

Evidence tier: Clinical observational evidence; study design and disease-specific subgroup sizes should be reviewed carefully.

Importance score: 6.5/10

Key limitations:

- Autoimmune and connective-tissue diseases are heterogeneous in diagnosis, severity, organ involvement, and activity.
- Selection bias is likely because clinicians may avoid radiotherapy in the highest-risk patients.
- Modern fractionation and planning reduce toxicity but may not eliminate risk in active systemic sclerosis or severe disease.
- Small disease-specific subgroups can obscure uncommon but serious toxicity.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Autoimmune disease is usually a relative rather than absolute contraindication to breast radiotherapy. This paper may help with individualized counselling, especially regarding disease activity, systemic sclerosis, field size, hypofractionation, cardiac and lung exposure, and the role of rheumatology input.

### 4. Nonstop Gated Cone-Beam CT for Respiratory-Gated Pancreatic Radiotherapy Using Dual-Domain Deep-Learning Reconstruction

Citation: Yu M, et al. Nonstop gated cone-beam computed tomography for respiratory-gating pancreatic cancer radiotherapy using dual-domain deep learning reconstruction. Physics and Imaging in Radiation Oncology. Published online July 15, 2026.[^4]

Journal/source: Physics and Imaging in Radiation Oncology  
Publication type: Peer-reviewed AI image-reconstruction technical validation study  
One-line takeaway: A dual-domain deep-learning method was developed to reconstruct gated CBCT without repeatedly stopping acquisition during respiratory-gated pancreatic radiotherapy.

Evidence tier: Technical imaging validation; no demonstrated patient-outcome benefit.

Importance score: 6.5/10

Key limitations:

- Image similarity and reconstruction metrics do not prove safe target localization.
- Deep-learning reconstruction can suppress, distort, or hallucinate clinically relevant anatomy.
- External validation across machines, breathing patterns, body habitus, and institutions is essential.
- The impact on registration accuracy, treatment time, intrafraction motion, and delivered dose requires prospective study.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Pancreatic motion management is resource-intensive, and better gated volumetric imaging could improve efficiency and confidence. The practical question is whether reconstructed images support accurate soft-tissue matching and safe treatment under real-world respiratory irregularity. This remains a promising technical tool rather than a validated autonomous workflow.

### 5. Reirradiation of Locally Recurrent Prostate Cancer: Prospective Evidence and Delineation and Planning Strategies

Citation: Reirradiation of locally recurrent prostate cancer: a review of prospective evidence and delineation and planning strategies. Clinical and Translational Radiation Oncology. Published online July 14, 2026.[^5]

Journal/source: Clinical and Translational Radiation Oncology  
Publication type: Peer-reviewed review article  
One-line takeaway: The review summarizes prospective salvage-reirradiation evidence and practical approaches to target definition, dose selection, and organ-at-risk protection.

Evidence tier: Review of prospective but heterogeneous, generally nonrandomized studies.

Importance score: 6.5/10

Key limitations:

- Prospective evidence remains limited and lacks definitive randomized comparisons with surgery, ablation, or systemic management.
- Techniques, dose schedules, focality, imaging, and toxicity definitions vary substantially.
- Late urinary and rectal toxicity may emerge beyond the follow-up of smaller series.
- Safe practice depends on reliable prior-dose reconstruction and experienced multidisciplinary selection.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Local salvage after prior prostate radiotherapy is increasingly encountered in the PSMA PET and MRI era. This review is useful for residents and clinicians considering salvage SBRT or brachytherapy. Key issues include biopsy confirmation, exclusion of metastatic disease, baseline urinary function, recurrence location, interval from prior treatment, urethral and rectal dose, cumulative uncertainty, and realistic counselling about severe late toxicity.

## Save for Later

### 6. Pulmonary Changes After Single-Dose Interstitial Brachytherapy for Lung Tumors

Citation: Al Mansoor J, Rashid A, Haddad H, Hermani H, Bischoff P, Kovács A, Pinkawa M. Pulmonary changes after single dose interstitial brachytherapy for lung tumors. Brachytherapy. Published online July 14, 2026.[^6]

Journal/source: Brachytherapy  
Publication type: Peer-reviewed clinical imaging and toxicity study  
One-line takeaway: The study characterizes post-treatment pulmonary imaging changes after single-fraction interstitial brachytherapy for lung tumours.

Evidence tier: Observational post-treatment imaging study.

Importance score: 5.5/10

Key limitations:

- Interstitial lung brachytherapy is specialized and not widely available.
- Imaging changes may be difficult to distinguish from recurrence, infection, or other treatment effects.
- Cohort size, lesion selection, and follow-up may limit generalizability.
- The study does not establish comparative efficacy against SBRT or thermal ablation.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
This is useful for clinicians working with image-guided interstitial ablation or reviewing follow-up imaging after focal lung treatment. Recognizing expected temporal imaging changes may reduce false-positive recurrence calls, but interpretation should remain multidisciplinary.

### 7. Comparative Analysis of Stereotactic Radiosurgery and Microsurgery for Vestibular Schwannoma

Citation: Boyke AE, Al-Saadi T, Menaker SA, et al. Comparative analysis of stereotactic radiosurgery and microsurgery for vestibular schwannoma: a clinical and radiological evaluation. Journal of Neuro-Oncology. 2026;179(1):3.[^7]

Journal/source: Journal of Neuro-Oncology  
Publication type: Peer-reviewed comparative observational study  
One-line takeaway: Stereotactic radiosurgery and microsurgery were compared for tumour, hearing, neurologic, and radiographic outcomes in vestibular schwannoma.

Evidence tier: Nonrandomized comparative clinical study.

Importance score: 5.5/10

Key limitations:

- Treatment selection is strongly confounded by tumour size, symptoms, age, hearing, and patient preference.
- Retrospective comparisons cannot fully account for baseline differences.
- Follow-up duration is critical because schwannoma control and cranial-nerve outcomes evolve slowly.
- Results from a specialized centre may not generalize to all surgical or radiosurgical programs.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
The paper is helpful for CNS clinic counselling and multidisciplinary discussion. It reinforces that SRS is often an attractive option for appropriately sized tumours, while surgery remains important for large, symptomatic, compressive, or diagnostically uncertain lesions. Patient goals, hearing status, tumour anatomy, hydrocephalus risk, and centre expertise remain central.
