Weekly Radiation Oncology Literature Surveillance Digest: June 21–28, 2026

Coverage window searched: June 21–28, 2026

Scope searched: PubMed, arXiv, medRxiv, bioRxiv, IJROBP, Radiotherapy & Oncology, Practical Radiation Oncology, JCO, Lancet Oncology, Nature Medicine, Medical Physics, and Physics in Medicine & Biology.

Note: This is a best-effort literature surveillance digest, not a formal systematic review.

Bottom Line This Week

The most clinically meaningful item this week is the long-term JCOG1008 follow-up supporting weekly cisplatin plus postoperative radiotherapy as a reasonable alternative to every-3-week cisplatin in high-risk postoperative head and neck cancer.

The most operationally relevant items are the proton therapy selection paper in locally advanced pancreatic cancer, the salvage prostate brachytherapy article, and the esophageal chemoradiotherapy marrow-suppression risk model.

The most relevant AI/technical item is the SAM-based automated ITV generation preprint. It is interesting for 4DCT and motion-management workflows, but it remains hypothesis-generating and should not be treated as autonomous target definition.

Overall, this week is clinically moderate: useful for head and neck, reirradiation, proton selection, and toxicity prediction, but with no new AI or adaptive radiotherapy paper that should immediately change practice.

## Must Read

### 1. Long-Term Follow-Up of JCOG1008: Weekly Cisplatin With Postoperative Radiotherapy in High-Risk Head and Neck Cancer

Journal/source: Journal of Clinical Oncology
Publication type: Peer-reviewed randomized phase 2/3 long-term follow-up
DOI/link: [^1]

One-line takeaway:
Long-term follow-up further supports weekly cisplatin plus postoperative radiotherapy as a reasonable alternative to every-3-week cisplatin in high-risk locally advanced squamous cell carcinoma of the head and neck.

Evidence tier: High — randomized phase 2/3 trial with long-term follow-up
Importance score: 8.5/10

Key limitations:
Population and practice context matter: postoperative high-risk disease, cisplatin eligibility, renal function, hearing, neuropathy, and institutional supportive care all affect applicability. Weekly cisplatin is more convenient and often better tolerated, but adherence and cumulative dose still matter.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Highly relevant for head and neck rotations and postoperative chemoradiotherapy discussions. This is one of the more practice-facing papers this week.

### 2. EXTENDing the Role of Radiation in Oligometastatic Disease

Journal/source: Journal of Clinical Oncology
Publication type: Peer-reviewed editorial/commentary
DOI/link: [^2]

One-line takeaway:
A timely commentary on how metastasis-directed radiotherapy is being positioned within the evolving oligometastatic disease evidence base.

Evidence tier: Expert commentary, linked to randomized oligometastatic evidence
Importance score: 7/10

Key limitations:
Commentary rather than primary trial data. Oligometastatic radiotherapy remains disease-site-specific, systemic-therapy-dependent, and vulnerable to patient-selection bias.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Useful for framing SBRT in oligometastatic disease: not “SBRT for everyone,” but careful integration with systemic therapy, imaging, prognosis, and trial evidence.

## Worth Skimming

### 3. Induction Chemotherapy as Biological Selection Before Proton Radiotherapy in Locally Advanced Pancreatic Cancer

Journal/source: Clinical Oncology
Publication type: Peer-reviewed clinical outcomes study
DOI/link: [^3]

One-line takeaway:
Induction chemotherapy may help select patients with locally advanced pancreatic cancer who are most likely to benefit from subsequent proton radiotherapy.

Evidence tier: Clinical outcomes study; likely retrospective or nonrandomized
Importance score: 6.5/10

Key limitations:
Selection bias is central: patients who do well on induction chemotherapy are biologically different from those who progress early. Proton access, dose/fractionation, systemic therapy, and surgical conversion criteria may not generalize across centres.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Relevant to GI radiation oncology, proton referral decisions, and the broader concept of using systemic therapy response as a filter before local intensification.

### 4. Efficacy and Safety of Salvage Brachytherapy for Local Recurrence After Radiotherapy for Prostate Cancer

Journal/source: Brachytherapy
Publication type: Peer-reviewed clinical outcomes article
DOI/link: [^4]

One-line takeaway:
Salvage brachytherapy remains an important focal/local salvage option after radiorecurrent prostate cancer, but toxicity and patient selection are critical.

Evidence tier: Clinical outcomes study; likely retrospective or prospective cohort rather than randomized evidence
Importance score: 6.5/10

Key limitations:
Reirradiation toxicity risk is substantial. Results depend heavily on recurrence localization, baseline urinary function, prior RT dose, interval since RT, biopsy confirmation, MRI/PSMA PET staging, and brachytherapy expertise.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Highly relevant for prostate reirradiation discussions. Worth saving for GU rotations because salvage options after prior RT are nuanced and multidisciplinary.

### 5. Developing and Validating a Risk Model for Severe Bone Marrow Suppression in Esophageal Cancer Treated With Radiotherapy or Chemoradiotherapy

Journal/source: Technology in Cancer Research & Treatment
Publication type: Peer-reviewed retrospective prediction-model study
DOI/link: [^5]

One-line takeaway:
A single-centre retrospective model used routine clinical and lab variables to predict severe bone marrow suppression during esophageal RT/CRT.

Evidence tier: Retrospective internally validated prediction model
Importance score: 6/10

Key limitations:
Single-centre; retrospective; internally validated only; very high apparent AUC raises concern for overfitting; does not replace dosimetric marrow analysis or prospective validation.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Useful for toxicity prediction and supportive-care planning in esophageal chemoradiotherapy, but not yet ready as a decision-making standard.

---

### 6. Parameter-Efficient Adaptation of SAM 3 for Automated ITV Generation From 4DCT Images

Journal/source: arXiv
Publication type: Preprint
DOI/link: [^6]

One-line takeaway:
A lightweight SAM-based approach for 4DCT segmentation may improve automated ITV generation by using phase-coherent filtering and limited annotated data.

Evidence tier: Technical AI segmentation preprint
Importance score: 6/10

Key limitations:
Preprint only; limited validation; target definition is high-stakes; ITV quality depends on tumour visibility, phase sorting, motion artifacts, and clinician review. Good Dice scores do not prove clinical safety.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Relevant to lung SBRT, motion management, adaptive RT, and AI contouring workflows. Promising as a contouring assistant, not as autonomous ITV generation.

## Save for Later

### 7. Enhancing Heart and Lung Dose Reconstruction in Breast Cancer Radiotherapy Using Publicly Available Out-of-Field Dose Profiles

Journal/source: Physica Medica
Publication type: Peer-reviewed medical physics / dose reconstruction article
DOI/link: [^7]

One-line takeaway:
A dose-reconstruction method may improve estimation of heart and lung doses in breast radiotherapy, especially for historical or epidemiologic datasets.

Evidence tier: Medical physics methods study
Importance score: 5.5/10

Key limitations:
Dose reconstruction is useful for research and late-effects modelling, but does not directly alter modern planning unless validated against contemporary techniques and patient-specific anatomy.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Relevant for breast survivorship, cardiac toxicity research, and epidemiology. Lower immediate clinical impact than treatment trials.

### 8. Impact of Consolidation-to-Tumor Ratio on Prognosis After Radiotherapy

Journal/source: Advances in Radiation Oncology
Publication type: Peer-reviewed clinical outcomes article
DOI/link: [^8]

One-line takeaway:
Consolidation-to-tumor ratio may help risk-stratify prognosis after radiotherapy, likely in a thoracic oncology context.

Evidence tier: Clinical outcomes / prognostic factor study
Importance score: 5/10

Key limitations:
Prognostic imaging biomarkers are often context-dependent and may not independently change treatment selection without prospective validation.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Potentially useful for lung SBRT or thoracic RT risk stratification, but not practice-changing without clearer treatment implications.

DOI/link: [^9]

A review of thoracic radiotherapy-related cardiac toxicity in lung cancer.
