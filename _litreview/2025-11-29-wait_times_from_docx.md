# Wait Times from Lumpectomy to Adjuvant Radiotherapy in Canada  
---

# Introduction

Timeliness in initiating adjuvant radiotherapy (RT) following breast-conserving surgery (BCS) is a critical determinant of oncologic quality and health-system performance. For early-stage breast cancer patients who do not receive chemotherapy, clinical guidelines universally recommend initiation of RT within **6–12 weeks** after lumpectomy. Delays beyond this window are associated with progressively higher risks of local recurrence and, in extreme cases, inferior survival outcomes. 

Despite substantial investments in radiotherapy infrastructure nationwide, my anecdotal observations suggest that some patients are beginning adjuvant RT **16–17 weeks** after surgery, substantially outside guideline boundaries. Conversely, **British Columbia**, which experienced a severe capacity crisis in 2022–2023, has since undergone a major system recovery.

This analysis synthesizes guideline evidence, pathway timing data, and provincial operational performance to evaluate whether prolonged lumpectomy-to-RT intervals represent isolated outliers or a broader structural issue within Canadian cancer systems.

---

# 1. Clinical Guidelines and the Evidence Base for Timely RT

## 1.1 Guideline Expectations

Across Canadian and international sources, there is strong convergence around an outer limit of **12 weeks** for RT initiation after BCS in the absence of chemotherapy:

- **Canadian national guidelines**: “As soon as possible and not later than **12 weeks**” post-lumpectomy.  
- **NCCN**: Initiate RT **6–8 weeks** after surgery when no systemic therapy intervenes.  
- **European (ESTRO / Dutch)**: Ideal **4–6 weeks**; maximum **12 weeks**.  
- **Operational benchmark (CPAC/CIHI)**: 90% of patients treated within **28 days** of readiness—an operational proxy but *not* a clinically defined interval.

### Clinical Rationale  
- Local recurrence risk increases with each month of delay beyond approximately eight weeks.  
- Delays **>12 weeks** consistently worsen local control across meta-analyses and cohort studies.  
- Very prolonged delays (**>20 weeks**) have demonstrated negative impacts on recurrence-free and overall survival in Canadian population data.

Thus, the evidence supports a biologically and clinically meaningful window for initiating adjuvant RT after BCS.

---

# 2. Conceptualizing the Treatment Pathway  
### Why System Metrics and Clinical Metrics Diverge

The full patient pathway includes multiple sequential intervals:

1. **Surgery → Referral**  
2. **Referral → Radiation Oncology Consult**  
3. **Consult → CT Simulation**  
4. **Simulation → RT Ready Date**  
5. **Ready → First Fraction**

Provincial dashboards, CIHI indicators, and most public wait-time frameworks measure **only the final segment** (Ready→Start). These operational metrics reflect treatment capacity but do **not** capture the clinically relevant delay from **surgery to RT start**.

### Resulting Measurement Blind Spot
- A province may appear to meet all wait-time benchmarks, even while patients experience **substantial delays upstream** (especially referral→consult).
- This blind spot is currently consequential in Alberta.

---

# 3. Alberta: Consultation Bottleneck and Its Systemic Consequences

## 3.1 Historical Performance (Pre-2020)

Prior to the COVID-19 era, Alberta consistently met or exceeded pan-Canadian RT benchmarks:

- **98%** of patients started RT within **28 days** of readiness (2020).  
- **64%** started within **2 weeks**.  
- Implied surgery→RT intervals (for direct-to-RT patients): **6–10 weeks**.

These metrics were sustained by substantial expansion of regional RT centres between 2010–2020.

## 3.2 Current State (2023–2025): A Front-End Access Crisis

Despite continued strong performance in the Ready→Start segment, Alberta now faces **prolonged access delays** to radiation oncology consultation:

- **Referral → consult:** up to **12–13 weeks**, versus a 4-week benchmark.  
- **Once ready:** 90% still treated within ≤28 days.

### Mechanistic Explanation
The **consult bottleneck**, not linac capacity, is the dominant driver of extended surgery→RT intervals. When a patient waits 12–13 weeks for consultation, even a high-functioning treatment service cannot prevent total waits from exceeding guideline limits.

### Resulting Clinical Interval  
Surgery (~week 0) → referral (variable) → consult (week 12–13) → planning (1–2 weeks) → RT start (week 14–17).

**Thus, 16–17 week starts are systemically predictable given current access constraints.**

## 3.3 Structural Drivers
- Rising cancer incidence (+40% since 2013).  
- Only 20% growth in oncology workforce over the same period.  
- Clinic room constraints, particularly at the Cross Cancer Institute (Edmonton).  
- Pandemic-driven surgical delays creating fluctuating referral surges.

Alberta therefore demonstrates a **front-end access failure** embedded within an otherwise functional treatment-capacity system.

---

# 4. British Columbia: Capacity Crisis, Cross-Border Treatment, and Recovery

## 4.1 Crisis Period (2022–2023)

BC Cancer experienced system-wide deterioration due to therapist shortages, oncologist vacancies, and rising volumes:

- Ready→Start performance worsened to **~65–70%** within 28 days.  
- Many breast patients initiated RT **>12–16 weeks** post-surgery.  
- Over **1,100 patients** were treated in Washington State to mitigate delays.

## 4.2 Recovery (2024–2025)

Following aggressive investment:

- **76%** treated within 4 weeks (mid-2024).  
- **93%** treated within 4 weeks (Feb 2025)—exceeding national benchmarks.  
- Cross-border outsourcing ended.

### Key Contrast With Alberta
- **BC’s deficit was treatment capacity** (linacs & therapists).  
- **Alberta’s deficit is pre-treatment access** (physician consult availability).

BC demonstrates that large-scale system remediation can reverse severe wait-time deterioration within ~18–24 months.

---

# 5. Comparing Alberta and BC Through a Health-Systems Lens

| Dimension | Alberta | British Columbia |
|----------|---------|------------------|
| **Primary bottleneck** | Consult access | Treatment capacity |
| **Ready→Start performance** | Strong (≤28 days) | Recovered to strong (93% ≤28 days) |
| **Surgery→RT performance** | Suboptimal (14–17 weeks in many cases) | Improved; now closer to guideline windows |
| **System trajectory** | Worsening consult delays | Rapid recovery after crisis |
| **Underlying driver** | Workforce & clinic constraints | Insufficient RT throughput |

Both systems highlight the importance of distinguishing **operational metrics** from **clinically meaningful intervals**.

---

# 6. Clinical Interpretation: Are 16–17 Week Delays Acceptable?

Based on the guideline synthesis and evidence review:

### 6.1 Within-Historical Norms?  
No. Alberta’s historical median was ~8–10 weeks.

### 6.2 Within Current System Performance?  
Yes—these waits are systemically generated by the consult backlog.

### 6.3 Clinically Acceptable?  
No. These delays exceed the **12-week** upper limit endorsed by all major guidelines and carry increased risk of local recurrence.

### 6.4 Do They Warrant QI Investigation?  
Yes. Any centre with routine post-lumpectomy RT initiation beyond 12 weeks should conduct pathway-level QI analysis to identify bottlenecks.

---

# 7. Obtaining Accurate Surgery→RT Data for Local QI

Clinicians or trainees can access true interval data via:

- **Alberta:** Cancer Care Alberta Analytics / Alberta Cancer Registry  
- **BC:** BC Cancer Surveillance & Outcomes Division  

Recommended minimum dataset:

- Date of lumpectomy  
- Date of referral  
- Date of RO consultation  
- CT simulation date  
- RT start date  

Key calculated intervals:

- Surgery → consult  
- Consult → RT start  
- **Surgery → RT start (primary clinical metric)**

Interpretation thresholds:

- **6–12 weeks:** guideline-concordant  
- **12–16 weeks:** delayed  
- **>16 weeks:** operational/system failure  
- **>20 weeks:** associated with poorer survival in selected cohorts  

Because provincial dashboards lack surgery→RT metrics, such local audits often reveal hidden bottlenecks unnoticed by system leaders.

---

# 8. Implications for Oncology Practice and Health-System Planning

1. **Guideline adherence is no longer guaranteed by meeting RT operational benchmarks.**  
   Strong Ready→Start performance can coexist with clinically unacceptable surgery→RT intervals.

2. **Consult access is a critical rate-limiting step** in modern cancer pathways.  
   Workforce planning must consider both physician FTEs and clinic infrastructure.

3. **British Columbia shows that aggressive investment can restore performance** even after crisis-level deterioration.

4. **Local QI initiatives should incorporate surgery→RT intervals** as core quality indicators.

5. **Patients starting RT 16–17 weeks post-surgery are not outliers** in Alberta’s current system—they represent structural constraints, not stochastic variation.

6. **Persistent delays risk worsening population-level cancer outcomes** via increased local recurrence, particularly among higher-risk tumor subtypes.

---

# Conclusion

In Canada’s post-pandemic oncology landscape, lumpectomy-to-radiotherapy intervals are increasingly shaped by health-system design rather than RT delivery capacity alone. Alberta’s prolonged waits are driven primarily by delayed consultation access, whereas British Columbia’s earlier crisis was rooted in treatment throughput constraints. Both scenarios underscore the need for accurate measurement of the **entire surgical-to-treatment pathway**, not merely the operational Ready→Start interval.

Effective quality improvement and system planning require acknowledging—and directly addressing—the structural bottlenecks that lead to 16–17 week delays. Maintaining national cancer outcomes depends on ensuring that the interval between lumpectomy and adjuvant RT remains within the evidence-based window of **6–12 weeks**, thereby aligning clinical practice with established guidelines and optimizing patient outcomes.
