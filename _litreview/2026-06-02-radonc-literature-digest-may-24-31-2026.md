# Weekly Radiation Oncology Literature Surveillance Digest: May 24-31, 2026

Preamble: 
I am one month away from my radiation oncology residency at U of A, starting July so I wanted to prime my mind for rad onc topics. Also... I wanted to experiment with AI Agents via scheduling.

This is a self curated list using ChatGPT5.5-Thinking model. I created a scheduled task, i.e. a basic AI Agent to provide me with topics in rad onc that interest me. Topics include: adaptive radiotherapy, image guidance, AI contouring, treatment planning, QA, toxicity prediction, SBRT, reirradiation, and major oncology trials.

## Must Read

### 1. Detsky JS et al. MRI-guided adaptive radiotherapy for high-grade glioma

Journal: The Lancet Oncology, 2026
Publication type: Peer-reviewed clinical trial
Link/DOI: [^1]

One-line takeaway:
Prospective phase II evidence suggests MRI-guided adaptive RT for high-grade glioma is feasible and safe enough to take seriously, but not yet a universal replacement for conventional workflows.

Evidence tier: Prospective single-centre, single-arm, non-inferiority phase II trial
Importance score: 9/10

Key limitations:
Likely selected patients; single-centre design; resource-intensive MRI-linac workflow; unclear generalizability to centres without MRI-guided adaptive platforms; mature survival, neurocognitive, pseudoprogression, and toxicity outcomes remain important.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Highly relevant to CNS adaptive RT, margin reduction, MRI-linac clinical value, and the broader question of whether adaptive radiotherapy can meaningfully improve therapeutic ratio in infiltrative brain tumours.

---

### 2. Brunt AM et al. Hypofractionated breast radiotherapy for 1 week versus 3 weeks

Journal: The Lancet Oncology, 2026
Publication type: Peer-reviewed randomized trial / long-term outcomes
Link/DOI: [^2]

One-line takeaway:
Long-term FAST-Forward evidence continues to support 1-week breast RT as a major patient-convenience and system-capacity win in appropriately selected patients.

Evidence tier: Multicentre randomized phase III non-inferiority trial with long-term follow-up
Importance score: 9/10

Key limitations:
Technique details, boost use, nodal RT, reconstruction status, and patient selection matter. This should not be simplistically interpreted as “1 week for everyone.”

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Practice-changing for breast fractionation, treatment capacity planning, patient-centred care, and reducing treatment burden.

---

### 3. Siva S et al. Ultra-hypofractionated stereotactic ablative body radiotherapy for primary renal cell carcinoma: 5-year pooled FASTRACK outcomes

Journal: The Lancet Oncology, 2026
Publication type: Peer-reviewed prospective pooled analysis
Link/DOI: [^3]

One-line takeaway:
Long-term pooled FASTRACK data strengthen SABR as a serious non-invasive option for selected patients with primary RCC, especially those who are medically inoperable.

Evidence tier: Prospective pooled analysis of phase I/II trial data
Importance score: 8.5/10

Key limitations:
Non-randomized data; selection bias toward medically inoperable/non-surgical patients; limited direct comparison with surgery, ablation, or active surveillance.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Important for SBRT/SABR clinic, kidney motion management, dose constraints, renal function preservation, and multidisciplinary RCC discussions.

---

### 4. Kirby AM et al. Real-world heart and lung doses from 30,000 National Health Service breast radiotherapy patients

Journal: The Lancet Oncology, 2026
Publication type: Peer-reviewed large-scale dosimetric audit
Link/DOI: [^4]

One-line takeaway:
A large real-world NHS dosimetry audit provides useful benchmarks for cardiac and lung exposure in modern breast radiotherapy.

Evidence tier: Population-level observational dosimetric study
Importance score: 8/10

Key limitations:
Dosimetric outcomes are not the same as clinical events; technique heterogeneity and incomplete patient-level clinical risk data may limit causal interpretation.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Useful for breast planning QA, DIBH justification, institutional audit, cardiac dose reduction, and patient counselling.

## Worth Skimming

### 5. Riggenbach E et al. Uterine ultra-mover in cervical cancer radiotherapy: retroversion and the rationale for online adaptive planning

Journal: Practical Radiation Oncology, 2026
Publication type: Peer-reviewed case-based / technical clinical report
Link/DOI: [^5]

One-line takeaway:
A striking cervical cancer adaptive RT case showing how major uterine positional change can challenge static planning and standard margins.

Evidence tier: Case-based technical report
Importance score: 7/10

Key limitations:
Not outcomes evidence; may represent an unusually dramatic anatomy-change scenario; does not resolve cost, staffing, or throughput challenges of online adaptive RT.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Useful for cervix adaptive RT, CBCT-based decision-making, and understanding when anatomy-of-the-day meaningfully matters.

---

### 6. Yock AD et al. Multi-institutional validation of a practical, reconfigurable QA approach for online adaptive radiation therapy

Journal: Practical Radiation Oncology, 2026
Publication type: Peer-reviewed multi-institutional technical validation
Link/DOI: [^6]

One-line takeaway:
Addresses a real bottleneck in online adaptive RT: QA methods must be fast, robust, and deployable across institutions.

Evidence tier: Multi-institutional technical validation study
Importance score: 7.5/10

Key limitations:
QA validation does not prove improved patient outcomes; implementation may depend heavily on local equipment, staffing, software, and institutional workflow.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
High operational relevance for adaptive RT programs, particularly CBCT-guided online adaptive platforms and daily reoptimization workflows.

### 7. Han Y et al. Clinical implementation of dual-isocenter MR-guided adaptive radiotherapy

Journal: Practical Radiation Oncology, 2026
Publication type: Peer-reviewed technical implementation / clinical workflow report
Link/DOI: [^7]

One-line takeaway:
Practical implementation paper showing how MR-guided adaptive RT can be extended to larger or more complex targets requiring dual isocentres.

Evidence tier: Technical implementation and feasibility report
Importance score: 7/10

Key limitations:
Likely institution-specific; feasibility does not guarantee broad scalability; treatment time, staffing, and QA burden remain major considerations.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Helpful for adaptive workflows, complex abdominal/pelvic targets, and understanding MR-linac operational constraints.

---

### 8. Shinde N et al. Lattice peak optimization: a mixed-integer framework for geometry-adaptive lattice radiotherapy

Journal: Physics in Medicine & Biology, 2026
Publication type: Peer-reviewed medical physics methods paper
Link/DOI: [^8]

One-line takeaway:
Presents an optimization framework for lattice RT peak placement that adapts to patient geometry rather than relying on heuristic spatial patterns.

Evidence tier: Medical physics optimization / methods study
Importance score: 6.5/10

Key limitations:
Lattice RT remains biologically and clinically unsettled; optimization elegance may outpace clinical evidence; clinical benefit and patient selection remain uncertain.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Worth knowing for advanced planning, spatially fractionated RT, and future trial design.

---

### 9. Wang B et al. AI-assisted decision-making for clinical assessment of auto-segmented contour quality

Source: arXiv, 2025
Publication type: Preprint
Link/DOI: [^9]

One-line takeaway:
Bayesian ordinal classification with uncertainty thresholds may reduce manual review burden for auto-contours, especially in online adaptive RT.

Evidence tier: Retrospective AI/QA validation; preprint
Importance score: 7/10

Key limitations:
Rectum/prostate-focused; surrogate labels may not capture clinically meaningful contour failure modes; manual-label fine-tuning may not generalize; requires prospective clinical validation.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Very relevant to contour QA, adaptive RT safety, and “AI as triage” rather than blind automation.

## Save for Later

### 10. Wang Z et al. Test-time adaptation-driven patient-specific auto-segmentation for CBCT-guided online adaptive radiotherapy in cervical cancer

Journal: Journal of Applied Clinical Medical Physics, 2026
Publication type: Peer-reviewed technical AI segmentation study
Link/DOI: [^10]

One-line takeaway:
Patient-specific test-time adaptation may improve auto-contouring under changing pelvic anatomy in cervical adaptive RT.

Evidence tier: Retrospective AI segmentation study
Importance score: 6.5/10

Key limitations:
Segmentation metrics may not translate into dosimetric or clinical safety; cervical anatomy is highly variable; external validation and prospective testing are essential.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Good methods paper for cervix online adaptive RT, CBCT segmentation, and AI-assisted contouring workflows.

### 11. Berchtold J et al. Prediction of VMAT gamma passing rates using 3D CNNs

Journal: Medical Physics, 2026
Publication type: Peer-reviewed technical ML/QA study
Link/DOI: [^11]

One-line takeaway:
CNN-based prediction of patient-specific QA pass rates could help triage plans in time-critical adaptive or high-throughput workflows.

Evidence tier: Retrospective technical ML/QA study
Importance score: 6/10

Key limitations:
Gamma pass rates are imperfect safety surrogates; model drift and institution-specific delivery systems are major concerns; clinical deployment would need careful monitoring.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Relevant to online adaptive QA, treatment planning automation, and physics workflow efficiency.

### 12. Genomic-adjusted radiation dose from bulk RNA sequencing for personalized radiotherapy

Source: bioRxiv, 2026
Publication type: Preprint
Link/DOI: [^12]

One-line takeaway:
Proposes transcriptomic personalization of radiation dose rather than uniform physical dose prescriptions.

Evidence tier: Translational/computational radiobiology preprint
Importance score: 6/10

Key limitations:
High risk of overfitting and retrospective biomarker optimism; clinical implementation would require prospective validation and actionable dose-adaptation trials.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Conceptually important for radiobiology and personalized RT, but not clinic-ready.

### 13. Loo P et al. FLASH reduces radiation-induced oral mucositis in a mouse model of Fanconi anemia

Source: bioRxiv, 2026
Publication type: Preprint / preclinical study
Link/DOI: [^13]

One-line takeaway:
Adds preclinical support for FLASH normal-tissue sparing in a clinically meaningful head and neck toxicity domain.

Evidence tier: Preclinical animal/experimental study; preprint
Importance score: 6/10

Key limitations:
FLASH translation remains constrained by beam delivery, field size, dosimetry, and tumour-control equivalence; oral mucositis models may not predict human benefit.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Useful for head and neck toxicity and FLASH background, but not practice-changing.

End-to-end deep learning framework generates deliverable single-arc VMAT prostate plans extremely quickly.

Evidence tier: Retrospective technical planning study; preprint
