Why Create an Importance Score?

One of the challenges of modern radiation oncology is not finding papers — it is deciding which papers deserve attention.

In a typical week, dozens of new publications appear across oncology, medical physics, artificial intelligence, imaging, and translational research journals. Most are incremental. Some are methodologically weak. A few may eventually change practice.

When preparing a weekly literature surveillance digest, simply assigning a paper an "importance score" of 7/10 or 9/10 felt unsatisfying because the process was essentially a black box.

The obvious question is:

What makes one paper an 8/10 and another a 5/10?

This post describes the framework used to answer that question.

---

The Goal

The objective is not to identify the most highly cited paper or the most technically sophisticated paper.

Instead, the goal is:

**Estimate the importance of a paper to a practicing radiation oncology department over the next 1–5 years.**

This deliberately favors clinical utility and implementation over novelty alone.

---

The Six Domains

Each paper receives a score from 0 to 10 in six domains.

1. Clinical Impact (30%)

How much could this paper change patient outcomes or treatment decisions?

Score| Interpretation
0–2| Minimal clinical implications
3–5| Relevant to selected patients or niche situations
6–8| Likely to influence management decisions
9–10| Potential standard-of-care implications

Examples:

- Phase III randomized trial showing improved survival → 9–10
- New contouring tool → 3–5
- Mouse radiobiology study → 1–2

---

2. Evidence Strength (25%)

How trustworthy is the underlying evidence?

Score| Interpretation
0–2| Preclinical study, opinion piece, hypothesis
3–5| Retrospective or observational study
6–8| Prospective cohort, guideline, validation study
9–10| Large randomized trial or definitive guideline

---

3. Radiation Oncology Specificity (15%)

How directly relevant is this paper to radiation oncology practice?

Score| Interpretation
0–2| General oncology or adjacent field
3–5| Relevant to a minority of practitioners
6–8| Important to a major subspecialty
9–10| Relevant to most radiation oncologists

---

4. Practice Readiness (15%)

Could this realistically influence practice in the near future?

Score| Interpretation
0–2| Purely exploratory
3–5| Requires substantial future validation
6–8| Potential implementation within several years
9–10| Could influence practice immediately

This category intentionally separates interesting science from actionable science.

---

5. Generalizability (10%)

How widely applicable are the findings?

Score| Interpretation
0–2| Highly specialized technology or population
3–5| Applicable only in selected centres
6–8| Relevant to many departments
9–10| Broad applicability across practice settings

---

6. Novelty (5%)

Does this represent a genuinely new idea?

Score| Interpretation
0–2| Incremental update
3–5| Moderate advancement
6–8| Significant innovation
9–10| Potential paradigm shift

Novelty receives the smallest weight intentionally.

Interesting papers are not necessarily important papers.

---

The Formula

The composite importance score is calculated as:

Importance Score =
0.30 × Clinical Impact
+ 0.25 × Evidence Strength
+ 0.15 × RO Specificity
+ 0.15 × Practice Readiness
+ 0.10 × Generalizability
+ 0.05 × Novelty

Scores are rounded to one decimal place.

---

Worked Example

Single-Fraction Prostate SBRT

Domain| Score
Clinical Impact| 8
Evidence Strength| 7
RO Specificity| 10
Practice Readiness| 4
Generalizability| 6
Novelty| 9

Result:

0.30×8 +
0.25×7 +
0.15×10 +
0.15×4 +
0.10×6 +
0.05×9
= 7.6/10

The paper is highly interesting and potentially transformative, but insufficiently mature to justify a higher score.

---

What the Score Is Not

The score is not:

- citation count
- journal impact factor
- methodological elegance
- social media attention
- publication prestige

A technical AI paper in a high-impact journal may still receive a modest score if implementation barriers remain high.

---

Limitations

Any scoring system reflects the values of the person designing it.

An academic physicist, a department head, a community radiation oncologist, and a resident may reasonably assign different weights.

This framework reflects one perspective:

**Importance to a practicing Canadian radiation oncology department over the next 1–5 years.**

Others may reasonably disagree.

---

Future Directions

Future versions may separate:

- Scientific Importance
- Practice Impact
- Educational Value

This may better distinguish papers that are intellectually exciting from papers that change what clinicians do on Monday morning.

For now, a single weighted score provides a transparent compromise between those competing goals.
