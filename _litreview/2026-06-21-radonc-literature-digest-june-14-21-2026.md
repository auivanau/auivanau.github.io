Weekly Radiation Oncology Literature Surveillance Digest: June 14–21, 2026

Coverage window searched: June 14–21, 2026

Scope searched: PubMed, arXiv, medRxiv, bioRxiv, IJROBP, Radiotherapy & Oncology, Practical Radiation Oncology, JCO, Lancet Oncology, Nature Medicine, Medical Physics, and Physics in Medicine & Biology.

Note: This is a best-effort literature surveillance digest, not a formal systematic review.
---

Bottom Line This Week

The most clinically meaningful paper this week is the JAMA Oncology phase 1/2 nonrandomized clinical trial of single-fraction 19 Gy prostate SBRT in selected low- and intermediate-risk localized prostate cancer. It is not practice-changing on its own, but it is a serious signal that ultra-short prostate SBRT may be feasible in carefully selected patients.

The most practically useful guideline item is the DEGRO practical guideline update on breast regional nodal irradiation, which is immediately relevant to breast contouring, nodal target selection, and adjuvant RT decision-making.

The most operationally relevant physics/AI items are the IGRT practice survey, the adaptive dose accumulation uncertainty framework, and the SAM-based automated ITV generation preprint. These are workflow-relevant but mostly hypothesis-generating or implementation-focused rather than practice-changing.

## Must Read

### 1. Zilli T, Franzese C, Guckenberger M, et al. Single-Fraction Stereotactic Body Radiotherapy for Localized Prostate Cancer

Journal/source: JAMA Oncology
Publication type: Peer-reviewed phase 1/2 nonrandomized clinical trial
DOI/link: [^1]

One-line takeaway:
Single-fraction 19 Gy prostate SBRT with urethral sparing and intrafraction motion control appeared feasible and well tolerated in a small selected cohort.

Evidence tier: Prospective multicentre phase 1/2 nonrandomized clinical trial
Importance score: 8/10

Key limitations:
Small sample size; nonrandomized; selected low/intermediate-risk patients; specialized motion management and urethral-sparing workflow; longer follow-up needed for late GU/GI toxicity, biochemical control, and comparison with standard 5-fraction SBRT.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Important for prostate SBRT and future treatment de-escalation. Not enough to replace standard fractionation, but worth watching closely.

### 2. Borm K, Krug D, Corradini S, et al. DEGRO practical guidelines: radiotherapy of breast cancer—regional nodal irradiation

Journal/source: Strahlentherapie und Onkologie
Publication type: Peer-reviewed guideline / consensus review
DOI/link: [^2]

One-line takeaway:
Updated practical guidance for regional nodal irradiation in breast cancer, including indications and technical considerations.

Evidence tier: Guideline / expert consensus based on available evidence
Importance score: 7.5/10

Key limitations:
Guideline recommendations still depend on local practice, systemic therapy era, surgical approach, patient anatomy, and evolving hypofractionation evidence.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Useful for breast rotations, nodal contouring, RNI indications, postmastectomy RT discussions, and daily planning decisions.

## Worth Skimming

### 3. Hemon C, Lebret D, Nunes JC, et al. An Uncertainty Estimation Framework for Dose Accumulation in Adaptive Radiotherapy

Journal/source: arXiv
Publication type: Preprint
DOI/link: [^3]

One-line takeaway:
A proposed framework estimates uncertainty in deformable image registration–based dose accumulation for CBCT-guided adaptive radiotherapy in cervical cancer.

Evidence tier: Retrospective technical methods study; preprint
Importance score: 7/10

Key limitations:
Small dataset; preprint; focuses mainly on DIR uncertainty; does not fully solve virtual CT, contouring, clinical threshold, or outcome-validation uncertainty.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Very relevant to online adaptive RT because cumulative dose estimates are only useful if uncertainty is visible and clinically interpretable.

---

### 4. Vezirović M, et al. EURADOS survey on IGRT practices in European radiotherapy clinics

Journal/source: Physica Medica
Publication type: Peer-reviewed survey study
DOI/link: [^4]

One-line takeaway:
A European survey highlights variation in IGRT practice, relevant to standardization and safety in modern radiotherapy.

Evidence tier: Survey / practice-pattern study
Importance score: 6.5/10

Key limitations:
Survey data reflect reported practice, not necessarily observed performance; European context may not generalize globally; does not directly link IGRT variation to patient outcomes.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Useful for thinking about CBCT protocols, image matching, departmental QA, adaptive RT readiness, and practice standardization.

---

### 5. Song C. Parameter-Efficient Adaptation of SAM 3 for Automated ITV Generation from 4DCT Images

Journal/source: arXiv
Publication type: Preprint
DOI/link: [^5]

One-line takeaway:
A foundation-model adaptation approach is proposed for automated internal target volume generation from 4DCT imaging.

Evidence tier: Technical AI segmentation preprint
Importance score: 6.5/10

Key limitations:
Preprint; likely limited external validation; ITV generation is high-stakes and requires robustness to motion artifacts, phase sorting errors, tumour visibility, and clinician review.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Relevant to lung/liver SBRT and motion management. Interesting as a contouring assistant, not autonomous target definition.

---

### 6. FLASH radiotherapy preserves hepatic function and maintains metabolic homeostasis in a murine breast cancer model

Journal/source: Radiation Oncology
Publication type: Peer-reviewed preclinical study
DOI/link: [^6]

One-line takeaway:
In a mouse breast cancer model, FLASH-RT appeared to reduce liver injury markers compared with conventional dose-rate RT while maintaining tumour growth delay.

Evidence tier: Preclinical animal study
Importance score: 6/10

Key limitations:
Mouse model; single-fraction electron setup; translation to clinical breast RT, human liver dose, and real-world FLASH delivery remains uncertain.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Supports continued FLASH radiobiology work, but not clinically actionable yet.

## Save for Later

### 7. Rethinking radiotherapy through spatial fractionation

Journal/source: Nature Reviews Clinical Oncology
Publication type: Peer-reviewed review / perspective
DOI/link: [^7]

One-line takeaway:
A review discussing spatially fractionated radiotherapy as an evolving strategy for tumour control and normal-tissue sparing.

Evidence tier: Review / perspective
Importance score: 5.5/10

Key limitations:
Review-level evidence; spatial fractionation remains technically and biologically complex; clinical indications are still being defined.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Useful background for GRID/LATTICE/spatially fractionated RT concepts, especially for bulky or radioresistant tumours.

### 8. RapidBrachyMCTPS 3.0: recent updates and validation of a Monte Carlo brachytherapy treatment planning system

Journal/source: Physics in Medicine & Biology
Publication type: Peer-reviewed medical physics validation study
DOI/link: [^8]

One-line takeaway:
Updated validation of a Monte Carlo–based brachytherapy treatment planning system.

Evidence tier: Technical validation study
Importance score: 5.5/10

Key limitations:
Technical validation does not automatically imply clinical adoption; workflow integration, commissioning, speed, QA, and regulatory considerations matter.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Relevant to brachytherapy physics, dose calculation accuracy, and future model-based dose calculation workflows.

## Low Priority

### 9. Yan R, Dai G, Lin Y, et al. Predicting Post-Radiotherapy Epigenetic Age Acceleration

Journal/source: Cancer Medicine
Publication type: Peer-reviewed biomarker / modelling study
DOI/link: [^9]

One-line takeaway:
Explores prediction of post-radiotherapy epigenetic age acceleration, a potential marker of treatment-related biological aging.

Evidence tier: Translational modelling study
Importance score: 4.5/10

Key limitations:
Biomarker endpoint rather than clinical toxicity endpoint; uncertain generalizability; unclear how results should change RT planning today.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Interesting for survivorship and late-effects research, but low immediate clinical utility.

### 10. Liao Z, et al. Application of delta radiomics based on cone-beam CT for predicting radiotherapy efficacy in nasopharyngeal carcinoma

Journal/source: Strahlentherapie und Onkologie
Publication type: Peer-reviewed retrospective radiomics study
DOI/link: [^10]

One-line takeaway:
CBCT-based delta radiomics may help predict response in nasopharyngeal carcinoma.

Evidence tier: Retrospective radiomics study
Importance score: 4.5/10

Key limitations:
Radiomics studies are vulnerable to overfitting, scanner/protocol variation, segmentation dependence, and poor external reproducibility.

Practical radiation oncology relevance:
Conceptually relevant to adaptive RT and response prediction, but not ready for treatment adaptation decisions.
