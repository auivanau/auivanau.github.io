## Weekly Radiation Oncology Literature Surveillance Digest: June 7–14, 2026

**Coverage window searched:** June 7–14, 2026  
**Scope:** PubMed, arXiv, medRxiv, bioRxiv, IJROBP, Radiotherapy & Oncology, Practical Radiation Oncology, JCO/ASCO publications, Lancet Oncology, Nature Medicine, Medical Physics, and Physics in Medicine & Biology.

**Note:** This is a best-effort literature surveillance digest, not a formal systematic review. Papers are prioritized for radiation oncology relevance, clinical importance, methodological strength, novelty, and practical usefulness.

---

## Bottom Line This Week

The most clinically meaningful item this week is the phase 3 Lancet Oncology trial of **aglatimagene besadenovec plus valacyclovir with EBRT in localized prostate cancer**, which showed improved disease-free survival without a major increase in severe toxicity.[^1] This is the closest thing to a practice-changing signal, although implementation would depend on regulatory approval, cost, workflow burden, patient selection, and longer-term outcomes.

The most operationally relevant physics/workflow item is the Radiotherapy & Oncology paper evaluating **PSQA device sensitivity and specificity**, because adaptive and highly automated treatment workflows increasingly depend on QA systems that can detect clinically meaningful errors rather than simply pass gamma thresholds.[^7]

The most relevant AI item is the ASCO Educational Book review on practical AI applications across oncology.[^8] It is not radiation-oncology-specific enough to change practice, but it is useful for framing AI tools as workflow aids requiring validation, oversight, privacy safeguards, and clinical accountability.

Overall, this week is **moderately clinically relevant**, with one strong prostate cancer randomized trial, several hypothesis-generating or implementation-focused studies, and no clearly practice-changing AI/autocontouring paper found in the searched window.

## Must Read

### 1. Aglatimagene besadenovec plus valacyclovir with EBRT for localized prostate cancer

**Citation:** DeWeese TL, Manzanera A, Sylvester J, et al. *Aglatimagene besadenovec (CAN-2409) with radiotherapy for patients with localised prostate cancer: a phase 3, multicentre, randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial.* Lancet Oncology. 2026;27(6):673–685.[^1]

**Journal/source:** *Lancet Oncology*  
**Publication type:** Peer-reviewed phase 3 randomized controlled trial  
**One-line takeaway:** Adding intraprostatic aglatimagene besadenovec plus valacyclovir to definitive EBRT improved disease-free survival in intermediate- and high-risk localized prostate cancer.

**Evidence tier:** High — phase 3, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicentre trial  
**Importance score:** 9/10

**Key limitations:**
- Disease-free survival is important, but longer follow-up is needed for metastasis-free survival, prostate cancer–specific survival, overall survival, late toxicity, and quality of life.
- The intervention adds intraprostatic injections and drug logistics to standard EBRT workflows.
- Industry funding and multiple author conflicts require careful interpretation.
- Generalizability to modern prostate workflows using PSMA PET staging, contemporary ADT intensification, SBRT, or focal boosting remains uncertain.

**Practical radiation oncology relevance:**  
This is highly relevant for prostate radiation oncology because it tests a biologic intensification strategy layered directly onto definitive EBRT. It may become important if confirmatory long-term outcomes and regulatory pathways support adoption. For now, it is a strong signal, not an immediate universal standard.

### 2. Hypofractionated breast radiotherapy for 1 week versus 3 weeks after breast-conserving surgery

**Citation:** Brunt AM, Cafferty FH, Kirby AM, et al. *Hypofractionated breast radiotherapy for 1 week versus 3 weeks after breast-conserving surgery.* Lancet Oncology. 2026;27(6):686–698.[^2]

**Journal/source:** *Lancet Oncology*  
**Publication type:** Peer-reviewed randomized trial / long-term breast radiotherapy outcomes  
**One-line takeaway:** The paper adds to the evidence base supporting shorter breast radiotherapy regimens after breast-conserving surgery.

**Evidence tier:** High — randomized breast radiotherapy evidence  
**Importance score:** 8/10

**Key limitations:**
- Applicability depends on patient selection, nodal status, boost use, systemic therapy context, and local comfort with ultrahypofractionation.
- Breast cosmesis, fibrosis, cardiac dose, and patient-reported outcomes remain critical when translating short-course regimens.
- The result reinforces an existing direction of travel rather than introducing a completely new paradigm.

**Practical radiation oncology relevance:**  
This is directly relevant to daily breast radiotherapy practice and supports continued normalization of shorter treatment courses where appropriate. The operational impact is substantial: shorter courses reduce patient burden and machine time, but require careful selection and consistent planning quality.

### 3. Long-term outcomes of SABR for primary kidney cancer

**Citation:** Siva S, et al. *Long-term outcomes of stereotactic ablative body radiotherapy for primary kidney cancer.* Lancet Oncology. 2026;27(6):711–720.[^3]

**Journal/source:** *Lancet Oncology*  
**Publication type:** Peer-reviewed long-term clinical outcomes study  
**One-line takeaway:** Long-term SABR data continue to support kidney SABR as a serious option for selected patients who are medically inoperable or seeking non-invasive local therapy.

**Evidence tier:** Moderate to high — long-term prospective/clinical outcomes evidence, but not necessarily phase 3 randomized evidence  
**Importance score:** 8/10

**Key limitations:**
- Kidney SABR is still more niche than surgery or ablation in many centres.
- Patient selection is key, especially tumour size, location, baseline renal function, and competing comorbidities.
- Comparative effectiveness against partial nephrectomy, thermal ablation, and active surveillance remains context-dependent.

**Practical radiation oncology relevance:**  
Very relevant for GU radiation oncology and SBRT programs. It strengthens the case for multidisciplinary discussion of kidney SABR, especially for patients who are poor surgical candidates. It also reinforces the need for robust motion management, image guidance, renal dose constraints, and long-term toxicity surveillance.

## Worth Skimming

### 4. PSA persistence in the PSMA PET era

**Citation:** Scharl S, Thamm R, et al. *PSA persistence in the PSMA PET era: reassessing a risk factor.* Radiotherapy and Oncology. 2026 Jun 12; online ahead of print.[^4]

**Journal/source:** *Radiotherapy and Oncology*  
**Publication type:** Peer-reviewed clinical outcomes / risk stratification study  
**One-line takeaway:** The paper revisits PSA persistence after prostatectomy in the context of modern PSMA PET imaging.

**Evidence tier:** Moderate — likely retrospective or observational clinical evidence  
**Importance score:** 7/10

**Key limitations:**
- PSMA PET changes staging and salvage treatment selection, which can make older PSA-persistence risk models less directly applicable.
- Management remains heterogeneous: prostate bed RT, nodal RT, metastasis-directed therapy, ADT, and systemic intensification may vary.
- Without randomized treatment assignment, outcome differences may reflect selection bias.

**Practical radiation oncology relevance:**  
Highly relevant to post-prostatectomy salvage radiotherapy decision-making. The main value is conceptual: PSMA PET may change what “PSA persistence” means clinically by separating occult local, nodal, and distant disease more accurately.

### 5. Long-term outcomes of prophylactic cranial irradiation in high-risk metastatic NSCLC

**Citation:** Cabrera-Miranda L, et al. *Long-term outcomes of prophylactic cranial irradiation in high-risk metastatic NSCLC: Final PRoT-BM analysis.* Radiotherapy and Oncology. 2026;219:111483.[^5]

**Journal/source:** *Radiotherapy and Oncology*  
**Publication type:** Peer-reviewed clinical outcomes analysis  
**One-line takeaway:** The final PRoT-BM analysis contributes to the ongoing question of whether selected high-risk metastatic NSCLC patients benefit from prophylactic cranial irradiation.

**Evidence tier:** Moderate — clinical outcomes analysis  
**Importance score:** 6/10

**Key limitations:**
- PCI in NSCLC remains controversial, especially in the era of MRI surveillance, immunotherapy, CNS-active targeted therapy, and improved salvage stereotactic radiosurgery.
- Neurocognitive toxicity and quality-of-life tradeoffs are central.
- The clinically relevant comparator may now be close MRI surveillance rather than historical usual care.

**Practical radiation oncology relevance:**  
Worth knowing for CNS and lung radiation oncology, but unlikely to broadly change practice without careful subgroup interpretation. The key issue is whether any reduction in brain metastases translates into meaningful patient-centered outcomes.

### 6. Sensitivity and specificity of PSQA devices in radiotherapy

**Citation:** May L, Baeza-Ortega JA, et al. *Sensitivity and specificity of PSQA devices in the detection of radiotherapy treatment errors.* Radiotherapy and Oncology. 2026;219:111493.[^7]

**Journal/source:** *Radiotherapy and Oncology*  
**Publication type:** Peer-reviewed medical physics / QA study  
**One-line takeaway:** PSQA tools should be judged by their ability to detect clinically meaningful errors, not only by pass rates or generic gamma metrics.

**Evidence tier:** Moderate — technical validation / QA methodology  
**Importance score:** 7/10

**Key limitations:**
- Device performance may not generalize across linacs, TPS models, treatment sites, modulation complexity, or institutional QA thresholds.
- Sensitivity/specificity frameworks are useful, but clinical consequence modeling remains difficult.
- Does not by itself define a universal QA standard.

**Practical radiation oncology relevance:**  
Important for departments moving toward automation, stereotactic treatments, and online adaptive workflows. It reinforces that QA should become more risk-based, error-specific, and clinically interpretable.

### 7. Artificial intelligence in oncology: practical applications across clinical care, scholarship, and translation

**Citation:** Hundal J, Vayal Veettil AA, Chung C, Hosseini M. *Artificial Intelligence in Oncology: Practical Applications Across Clinical Care, Scholarship, and Translation.* ASCO Educational Book. 2026;46(3):e517280.[^8]

**Journal/source:** *ASCO Educational Book*  
**Publication type:** Peer-reviewed review / educational article  
**One-line takeaway:** AI tools may help with documentation, decision support, literature review, patient communication, and remote monitoring, but require validation, oversight, privacy protection, and skepticism.

**Evidence tier:** Low to moderate — narrative/practical review rather than primary clinical evidence  
**Importance score:** 6/10

**Key limitations:**
- Broad oncology focus rather than radiation-oncology-specific implementation.
- Many AI use cases remain early, institution-dependent, and insufficiently validated.
- Risks include hallucination, bias, privacy leakage, inappropriate automation, and unclear medico-legal responsibility.

**Practical radiation oncology relevance:**  
Useful for framing AI in radiation oncology beyond autocontouring: documentation, guideline navigation, patient education, symptom monitoring, trial screening, and research workflows. However, this should be treated as a practical overview, not proof of clinical benefit.

### 9. RadTARGET trial protocol: focal boost with de-intensification in prostate cancer

**Citation:** *RadTARGET, a multicenter phase II randomized controlled trial evaluating focal radiotherapy boost with de-intensification of dose to non-suspicious prostate in intermediate- or high-risk prostate cancer.* medRxiv. 2026.[^10]

**Journal/source:** medRxiv  
**Publication type:** Preprint trial protocol  
**One-line takeaway:** A protocol exploring focal boost plus dose de-intensification reflects an important prostate radiotherapy trend, but it is not outcomes evidence yet.

**Evidence tier:** Low for practice change — protocol only  
**Importance score:** 5/10

**Key limitations:**
- Preprint and protocol only; no clinical outcome results.
- Requires accurate lesion localization, imaging registration, margin strategy, and robust QA.
- De-intensifying dose to “non-suspicious” prostate tissue assumes imaging can safely exclude clinically relevant disease.

**Practical radiation oncology relevance:**  
Conceptually relevant for MRI/PSMA-guided prostate planning, focal boosting, toxicity reduction, and adaptive or biologically guided radiotherapy. Not practice-changing until mature outcomes are available.

**Journal/source:** Peer-reviewed open-access literature  
**Publication type:** Conceptual / systems review  
**One-line takeaway:** Radiation oncology is usefully understood as a complex sociotechnical system, but the paper is more conceptual than immediately practice-changing.

**Evidence tier:** Low — conceptual review  
**Importance score:** 4/10

**Key limitations:**
- Limited direct clinical actionability.
- More useful for leadership, quality improvement, and systems design than for disease-site practice.
- Does not provide a specific validated intervention.

**Practical radiation oncology relevance:**  
Relevant for thinking about adaptive RT, AI implementation, QA, incident learning, and workflow redesign, but lower priority than clinical trials and concrete physics validation studies.
